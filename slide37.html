Economic Life and Justice

• Human-Centered Economy: "Economic life should be ordered to the service of man, and not man to the service of the economy."
• Universal Destination: God intended the earth and its goods for the use of all human beings.
• Private Property: Right to private property is legitimate but not absolute; includes social responsibility.
• Participation: All people should have access to meaningful work and economic opportunity.
• Global Perspective: Economic justice must be pursued at the international level.
"Economic activity is to be carried on according to its own methods and laws within the limits of the moral order, so that God\"s plan for mankind may be realized... Economic development must remain under man\"s determination and must not be left to the judgment of a few men or groups possessing too much economic power or of the political community alone or of certain more powerful nations."

Key Principles of Economic Justice

Principle Definition Application
Human Dignity Economy exists for people, not people for economy Worker rights, safe conditions, living wages
Common Good Economic activity should benefit all members of society Equitable distribution, access to basic goods
Subsidiarity Economic decisions at lowest appropriate level Local economies, appropriate regulation
Solidarity Commitment to the good of all, especially the poor Development aid, fair trade, debt relief
Stewardship Responsible use of resources for present and future Sustainable development, environmental protection
Contemporary Application:
The principles of economic justice outlined in Gaudium et Spes have been applied to issues like living wage campaigns, ethical investment strategies, and fair trade initiatives. For example, the Catholic Relief Services Fair Trade program helps ensure that producers in developing countries receive just compensation for their work, reflecting the document\"s emphasis on human dignity and global solidarity in economic relationships.
